The Raman Effect

June 25, 2020

Item #1
In 1928, Sir Chandrasekhara Venkata Raman (C.V. Raman) discovered, when a beam of coloured light entered a liquid, a fraction of the light scattered by that liquid was of a different color. He showed that the nature of this scattered light was dependent on the type of sample present.
Item #2
When light traverses a transparent material, some of the light that is deflected changes in wavelength. This phenomenon is now called Raman scattering and is the result of the Raman effect. In 1930, for this discovery, Sir C.V. Raman was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics.

Item #1
The Raman Effect is a very weak effect; only one in a million of the scattered light particles, or photons, actually exhibits the change in wavelength. The pattern of the change in wavelength is characteristic of the particular molecular species, and its intensity is proportional to the number of scattering molecules in the path of the light.
